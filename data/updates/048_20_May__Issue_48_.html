1. Teaching in Term 1

One of the biggest ‘calls’ everyone is expecting us to make is just how we will be teaching next academic year. We would all like certainty and yet that is in short supply at the moment as no one knows how the pandemic will progress. We don’t know what ‘social distancing’ will mean next academic year either in terms of the sizes of groups of people that can meet together and at what distance. We hope it will be very different from the current one per household meeting one from another household outside and at least 2 metres away from each other. Equally, we don’t know if students from outside the UK will be able to travel to London and what quarantine restrictions will be in place. Through all this, safety is our top priority and whilst we would all like to return to face-to-face teaching next academic year, we have to plan that much of our teaching will have to be delivered remotely and that the opportunities for face-to-face will be dictated to a significant extent by the social distancing constraints in place at the time. This is the dilemma being faced by universities across the world. We are talking with academics across the university so we can continue to deliver a high quality education and experience for our students next year.


We are planning to implement a range of social distancing measures to keep everyone safe on campus, if as we hope we can welcome students back on to campus in September. We hope that some small-group teaching, tutorials and laboratory or studio-based practicals can take place under these conditions, whilst of course understanding that not all staff will be able to travel to campus. As the universities of Manchester and Cambridge have already said, it is likely that face-to-face lectures and large group seminars will have to be delivered online. Each programme will be different and there is no “one size fits all” solution; with that in mind, we are working closely with departments to establish what teaching could be delivered under social distancing conditions and what would need to be delivered remotely.


It will of course be disappointing for everyone – staff and students alike – that we can’t meet face-to-face in all the ways that we are used to doing. However, lectures and large group seminars are only a part of a UCL education and students will still be able to enjoy a rich academic experience as well as opportunities to connect with London and the wider world.


Thank you for everything you are doing to prepare your teaching for what will be a very different year next year. We continue to follow the advice issued by the UK Government, Public Health England and the NHS to shape our plans. We will share more information with you as soon as it is available. In the meantime, please take a look at the teaching continuity website for guidance, tools, tips and training for delivering your teaching. The site is being updated regularly as new tools are developed.

3. Remote, not Distant at UCL: Spotlight on Mental Health Awareness Week

For Mental Health Awareness Week 2020, colleagues in UCL's HR Wellbeing team are encouraging you to perform one act of kindness either for yourself or for someone else every day and share it on Twitter or Instagram, tagging us @UCL_Wellbeing and using a hashtag #KindUCL. You can also email your pictures to us to add them to the Remote, not Distant virtual wall.


If you’re struggling during this difficult time, please do look at some of the free support that is available from UCL:


  • Employees can access Silvercloud, an online Cognitive Behavioural Therapy platform that offers self-paced programmes to help with stress, anxiety and depression.
  • You can also use our Employment Assistance Programme, Care First, if you need to speak to a professional in confidence.
  • Or sign-up for Care First’s webinar on stress, resilience and mental health, taking place tomorrow (Thursday 21 May) at 2-2.30pm.


The team have also launched a new toolkit for staff called Dealing with difficult situations, which includes resources and links to support staff who may be dealing with bereavement and loss, financial hardship, and challenging conversations at work.


If there’s something you’d like to see added to this resource, please get in touch by emailing your suggestions to remotenotdistantatucl@live.ucl.ac.uk.


4. Rapid Horizon 2020 COVID-19 response call: Deadline 11 June

The European Commission has made €122 million of funding available from its research and innovation programme, Horizon 2020, for urgently needed research into the coronavirus pandemic.


This complements the first rapid COVID-19 call, which closed earlier this year. The focus in this round is not to develop new diagnostics, therapeutics or vaccines, but rather to strengthen capacity to manufacture and deploy readily available solutions, as well as to improve understanding of the behavioural and socio-economic impacts of the pandemic.


The call is composed of five topics and the deadline for proposals is 11 June 2020. You can find out more on the Horizon 2020 website. If you have any queries please contact Dr Juliet A. Ellis at the European Research and Innovation Office (ERIO).

6. Support for staff and students

If you feel unwell and think that you may have coronavirus, please follow the latest Government advice. You can also find more information on the NHS website.

If you are worried about coronavirus or your wellbeing more generally, please speak to your line manager or check our list of resources to support staff during the outbreak. You can also find useful tips about looking after your wellbeing or if you’re feeling anxious on the Every Mind Matters website.

You can access free counselling services via the Employee Assistance Programme, Care First, which is available 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. Please telephone 0800 197 4510 to speak to an independent professional counsellor or information specialist in confidence.

Please advise any students who are worried to contact askUCL.
