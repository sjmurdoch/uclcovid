1. Increasing our efficiency through a new approach to procurement

The Procurement team has been working with colleagues across UCL to achieve better value for money for the goods and services that we buy. This includes looking at data to help us understand how we use company credit cards, expenses and the accounts payable system. We’ve also started a series of workshops bringing together colleagues from Procurement, Faculties, Professional Services and Vice-Provost offices to find new ways we can save money and improve our services at the same time.

      

Using these insights, and with your input, we’ve identified some potential ‘quick wins’ to reduce our non-pay expenditure. We’ve already started to improve our processes so it’s easier for staff and students to make responsible procurement decisions, which will help UCL save money and protect jobs.

  • You told us that it’s hard to identify suppliers, which makes it harder to follow best practice and means it can take a long time to procure items. We’re amending the approved supplier list to make this process easier for you.
  • The ideas we developed at our first workshop about reducing marketing expenditure led to a new “you said, we’ll do” commitment, to make sure we prioritise the strategic needs identified by faculties and standardise our supply chains where possible.
  • We’ve consolidated our media and social media tracking services and negotiated a new supplier contract to secure a better service that covers more media, while reducing spend by 30%.
  • We’re also working with the Sustainability team to explore ways we can reduce expenditure while meeting UCL’s objective to be net zero carbon by 2030, including improving how we recycle and reuse items. Did you know you can find desks, chairs, bookcases and more items via our internal furniture recycling scheme Warp It?

      

Thank you to everyone who’s contributed so far. Please continue to help by taking part in these workshops and sharing your suggestions for how and where UCL could reduce non-pay expenditure further. Future workshops will focus on travel, stationery, catering and IT. From opportunities to negotiate new contracts, to your feedback on the suppliers list, any idea – big or small – will make a difference.

                  

2. UCL reaches £1m fundraising milestone to tackle COVID-19

UCL has raised over £1m to drive the university’s response to the pandemic, through generous donations from our supporters, alumni and friends. This money will go directly towards UCL’s research activity, including developing ways to track, treat and prevent the virus; looking at how we can recover and rebuild society; and providing support for students in financial difficulties due to the pandemic.

    

More information about the campaign and how we’re using the fund to make a difference is available on the UCL website.

      

3. Restarting fieldwork

Fieldwork can restart from this week, as long as individuals wish to take part, and the activity:

  • Is in line with the plans for reopening buildings and travel advice;
  • Is supported by a risk assessment, which has been signed off by the relevant Dean.

      

You must ensure all fieldwork follows UK Government travel advice and any national or regional guidance at your destination. This includes the different rules operating in the four nations of the UK. The Foreign & Commonwealth Office is currently advising British nationals against all but essential international travel and UCL staff and students should not travel internationally on UCL business until 1 August 2020. This advice is being kept under review.

      

Fieldwork must also be reviewed in line with the insurance requirements, with any necessary actions considered in the risk assessment.

      

If you have any questions about restarting fieldwork, please speak to your manager or supervisor.

      

4. How UCL staff and students are studying the pandemic

This week UCL launched Virus Watch, one of the largest and most comprehensive studies of COVID-19 in the UK, with 42,500 people taking part to help us learn more about the next phase of the pandemic.


Participants will complete regular online symptom surveys and can download a movement tracking app so researchers can understand how activities outside of the house affect risk of infection. 10,000 of those taking part will also undergo blood tests, and nose and throat swabs if they become unwell, so we have a better idea of how long antibodies provide protection against the virus.

The study will help inform government planning, public health and the NHS response to COVID-19, and results will be regularly updated on the Virus Watch website.
