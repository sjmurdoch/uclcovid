Daily coronavirus (Covid-19)
update: issue 31


Dear colleagues,


It is now a full month since the country moved into lockdown and what was once unthinkable is now relatively routine for most of us. I hope you and your families remain well and are starting to adjust to some of the constraints that we are living with. As always, thank you for everything you are doing to support UCL and each other. I am deeply proud of the way our community has responded to this crisis and mobilised to support the national and global response effort to Covid-19.


Until now, our efforts have been directed at getting through the first phase of the Covid-19 crisis. Our priority from the very start has been to protect our community and I can’t reiterate enough how proud I am to lead a university of such committed staff and students.


However, it is now becoming increasingly clear that we will be living with this disease for some time and, along with all other Higher Education institutions both in the UK and globally, we will need to make significant adjustments to our activities and expectations in order to weather the challenges this presents. It is clear that there are major financial pressures looming for the sector and for UCL. These are completely unprecedented and – crucially – not yet easy to quantify.


International student recruitment brings in £6.9bn annually across the sector. Restrictions on movement will undoubtedly reduce this income, but we will not know until late summer quite how serious the impact will be.


Equally, many of the major funding streams for research are likely to be significantly reduced for at least the foreseeable future. Again, we cannot yet say with certainty what impact this will have at UCL, but initial indications are that this will create financial challenges for us.


We therefore need to plan for the prospect of a sizeable reduction in our income for at least the next year and probably well beyond, and to find ways to cope with this dramatic change in our reality.


I would like to reassure you that our collective aim is to bring the university through Covid-19 such that we ultimately remain academically excellent and retain our global top ten position. It is clear that we will only achieve this if we put our people first.


UCL’s success is entirely down to its researchers, teachers, students, professional services and support staff. We will do whatever we can to prioritise safety, stability, and the wellbeing of our university community.


It is a priority for me and the senior team to support our staff through what follows and to preserve jobs wherever we can. However, we have to be realistic about the scale of the challenges we are facing and will need to be prepared to take swift action to respond to issues as they unfold.


We have, of course, taken immediate action to cut costs: introducing a recruitment freeze (so we prioritise those who are already part of our community); pausing or cancelling significant elements of capital expenditure where we can do so without incurring costs and by cutting back drastically on non-pay expenditure so that we can build our cash reserves in the short-term.


You can really make a difference here: every pound we can save now will help to keep our options open when the precise extent of the financial challenge we face becomes clear.


Our guiding principle is that we will take any big decisions about controlling our spend with care and with humanity. We have already demonstrated how this can work in practice in the decisions that we have already taken in response to Covid-19, such as bringing forward the levelling-up of sick leave for our colleagues in catering, security and cleaning; our commitment to underwrite stipend provision for funded PhD students in their final year; and our extension of the hardship fund for all students. We also hope to be able to confirm further support for the PGR and postdoc community next week.


But we will need more than belt-tightening to bring us through what lies ahead. We worked with UUK and the Russell Group on proposals to Government for increased investment in research and in innovation in the short-term. Early indications are that the sector has not, as yet, convinced the Treasury of the need to act.


My view is that the entire future of the research capability of the country is at stake if sufficient funds cannot be found. We are heavily reliant on the cross-subsidy of research from international student tuition fees. If that income is not replaced, then research capacity will inevitably reduce to the point of becoming sub-critical and this puts the UK’s world-leading reputation for research and innovation under threat. Crucially, as a nation, we need to remain internationally competitive so that we will have a strong research base to facilitate the economic and social recovery of the UK post-pandemic.


Of course, we cannot rely too heavily on the Government providing sufficient investment to enable us to ride out this period. We hope that they will support the Higher Education sector and we will welcome that support as it comes. However, any investment will clearly be conditional on the sector taking every step to cut costs and mitigate losses, and this will include UCL. We are planning to draw down additional borrowing to ensure that our cash position remains as strong as possible, introducing a new approach to budgeting, and will temporarily set aside our contribution model until we are clearer about how much tuition fee income we will have for the next year.


You may be aware that other universities have announced an across-the-board pay freeze. This is something that we are not yet considering and we will exhaust all other options before we do so. This is in line with our commitment to put our people first.


In recognition of the fact that the financial implications of this crisis are far reaching and we have recently called for additional public money to support UCL, I have volunteered to take a pay reduction of 20% of my salary, which will be donated to the student hardship fund, for six months from August 1st. The Council’s Remuneration Committee has also agreed that we will open a Give as You Earn Scheme for the Senior Management Team and other members of staff who wish to contribute; more details of this to follow. These contributions will ensure we can continue to support our students in greatest need, whilst helping to protect our core funding for staff in the next academic year.


The Covid-19 pandemic has fundamentally changed how the university is operating and how we think of UCL as a community. However, those of us who have been working on the crisis response for several weeks are confident that we can find our way through with our global ambitions and position intact. It won’t be easy; it will require a lot of flexibility and a lot of hard work. There will be difficult decisions to take and we may not be able to work quite as we would wish for some time to come. But this is a moment for us to come together – and UCL is nothing if not highly collegial and co-operative.


We are fortunate to be able to draw on these traditions and these instincts as we face up to this moment of crisis. In that spirit, I am asking each and every one of you to contribute to this effort in whatever way you can – whether by exercising restraint over spending; by contributing your time and effort to the 20-21 planning and implementation work; or by working, teaching and researching in creative ways so that we are not constrained by straitened circumstances. We are a resilient and entrepreneurial university and I know that we will rise to the challenge. I thank you sincerely for your continued efforts and your support.


With very best wishes,


Professor Michael Arthur

UCL President & Provost

1. How you can help protect UCL's future

As the Provost says above, we have to consider the impact of the pandemic on UCL’s finances. Maintaining sufficient cash reserves to respond to urgent needs as they arise as well as our teaching and research capability is a key priority.


We are asking staff at every level to contribute in the following areas:


Non-pay expenditure

Please exercise maximum restraint over all non-pay expenditure, making purchases only when absolutely essential. For instance:


  • Use existing agreements with UCL’s preferred suppliers for common goods such as IT hardware and software, laboratory and workshop consumables and office supplies, as well as professional services including consultancy, advertising and media buying. By using our preferred suppliers, rather than making one-off purchases, you will help UCL achieve better value for money. More information about making purchases for UCL business is available on the Procurement Services website.
  • Please also delay where possible all large expenditures until after the pandemic has passed. This is particularly important for purchases that won’t lead to material benefits to UCL in the short term.
  • Where items are of low value and need to be delivered to a home location, please continue to follow UCL’s expenses guidance.


Next year’s budgeting process

The budgeting process will necessarily be different for next year because of all the uncertainties. We will do single-year budgeting, in which we set temporarily aside our usual processes through Term 1. By the end of Term 1, we will know how much tuition fee income we’ve achieved and can consider reverting to our normal process.


All budget holders will be contacted in the next few days and provided with further guidance. Please do your bit to support them through this. Thank you.

3. Season ticket refunds

If you are a season ticket holder, you can apply for a refund for your ticket from your train operator, including Transport for London. All train companies have suspended the administration fees on refundable train tickets.

National Rail is asking everyone to avoid unnecessary visits to ticket offices to minimise contact between passengers and staff. Please check with your train operator for instructions on how to apply for your refund remotely. More information about refunds and travel advice is available on the National Rail website.


If you have a season ticket loan with UCL and cancel your ticket, we do not expect the loan to be repaid immediately. Your monthly loan repayments will continue unchanged, in accordance with UCL’s Season Ticket Loan Policy. You may find it useful to use the refund to cover your travel costs through the purchase of short-term tickets until you are eligible to apply for another loan (approximately one year from the date of application for your current season ticket loan).

6. Support for staff and students

If you feel unwell and think that you may have coronavirus, please follow the latest Government advice. You can also find more information on the NHS website.

If you are worried about coronavirus or your wellbeing more generally, please speak to your line manager or visit our online support hub. You can also find useful tips about looking after your wellbeing or if you’re feeling anxious on the Every Mind Matters website.

You can access free counselling services via the Employee Assistance Programme, Care First, which is available 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. Please telephone 0800 197 4510 to speak to an independent professional counsellor or information specialist in confidence.

Please advise any students who are worried to contact askUCL.
