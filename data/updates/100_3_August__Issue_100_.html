1. Looking back at the last 100 working days

On 9 March 2020, we sent an email to all UCL staff with an update about coronavirus and its potential impact on UCL. The 100 working days that followed have seen incredible work and many inspiring moments from across the UCL community, as we have come together to study and tackle the virus, transform our teaching offer, and support our students and each other through this seismic change in how we live and work.

Colleagues from every Faculty, Vice Provost’s Office and Professional Services department have, at speed, first shut down over 200 buildings on campus and introduced a raft of new policies and resources to support staff and students as we were thrown into a world of remote studying and working. The pandemic has also brought with it questions about the number of students joining us in the autumn, and therefore also exceptional financial uncertainty. We have reduced expenditure across the university, in order to make sure UCL has a bright and sustainable future for years to come.

               

Throughout the lockdown, our essential workers remained onsite to keep our buildings and critical services running. Now, as the lockdown restrictions have eased, our community is focusing on reopening our buildings and spaces carefully and safely, ready for the start of the new academic year.

               

All of us have spent the last few months getting to grips with the challenges of remote working and becoming accustomed to meeting our colleagues on Microsoft Teams. For many, this has been against the backdrop of the additional demands of lockdown, including juggling childcare, home schooling, and looking after loved ones.

               

Teaching staff have worked tirelessly to reimagine our teaching offer and have successfully delivered 647 online exams over the summer. Although most of our students have gone home, they have continued to log-on from around the world and 147 student interns have been appointed to support the move of teaching materials online, including online clinical skills showing medical students how to inject oranges, suture bananas and even perform CPR on Spongebob Squarepants.

                

UCL’s community has stepped up to contribute to the national effort against the pandemic from the very beginning, with clinical staff volunteering to treat coronavirus patients and final-year medics graduating early to join the NHS as doctors. And researchers across UCL have continued to study the virus: from working to find a vaccine, to investigating the disproportionate effect of the virus on people from different ethnicities. UCL has directly influenced Government policy through our research and representation on the Scientific Advisory Group for Emergencies (SAGE). The UCL-Ventura breathing aid, produced in collaboration with Formula One engine maker Mercedes-AMG HPP, went from concept to manufacturing in just ten days, while the COVID-19 Social Study continues to give insight into the social and psychological impact on the pandemic on 70,000 people in the UK.

                

Every one of us has been hugely affected by the changes caused by COVID-19 and it’s unlikely that life will return to normal for some time yet. Despite this, the whole UCL community is still working hard to reopen our campus, organise teaching and support for the new academic year and make sure that UCL can continue to excel as one of the world’s leading universities.      

               

With very best wishes,

Fiona Ryland

Chief Operating Officer   

                                     

2. Travelling to and returning from Spain, Luxembourg and other countries which are not exempt travel corridors

On 27 July, the Foreign & Commonwealth Office (FCO) revoked the exempt travel corridor status for Spain at very short notice and did the same for Luxembourg soon afterwards. It is now clear that advice about the safety of travelling to countries can change with little advance warning. Please consider this possibility if you are travelling and factor this into your plans.


If you have travelled to a country and the FCO announces it is being removed from the exempt list, you must contact your line manager immediately. You may wish to consider returning to the UK immediately before restrictions are put in place.


From 3 August, where travel is not for agreed essential UCL work, the default position is for the member of staff to work remotely during their period of self-isolation if possible, by agreement with your line manager. If that is not possible, you should take any remaining annual leave entitlement followed by unpaid leave. This will apply whether the country was on or off the exemption list at the point of both booking and travel.

    

If you wish to travel to a country that is not on the exempt list, we advise you to discuss this with your line manager first. Where staff are required onsite, managers should consider the operational impact before agreeing annual leave to cover the trip and the two-week self-isolation period required on return. The impact on other staff wishing to take annual leave and/or the business needs of the department will need to be considered.

               

Before and while colleagues are travelling, it is essential that you check:

  • FCO coronavirus advice and travel advice for the applicable country;
  • You have appropriate travel insurance;
  • Answers to frequently asked questions about travel on the HR website.

4. Coronavirus: The Whole Story podcast – When will we have a vaccine?

More than 140 candidate vaccines for coronavirus are now being tracked by the World Health Organization. In this week’s episode, join host Vivienne Parry as she speaks to three key UCL researchers, Professor Sarah Edwards, Professor Eli Moore and Dr Sudaxshina Murdan, with expertise in biochemical engineering, bioethics and pharmaceutics to discover how close we are to an effective vaccine and how we can make sure everyone has access to it when it’s ready.
