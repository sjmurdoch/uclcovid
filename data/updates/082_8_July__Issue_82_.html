update: issue 82


Dear colleagues,


I hope that you and your loved ones are well and safe.


Last weekend saw the latest phase of national lockdown restrictions being lifted, with pubs, restaurants and galleries starting to reopen. Here at UCL, we are also beginning to work towards a return to something approaching normality, with the launch today of our roadmap for returning to campus. This sets out the plan and supporting guidance for reopening buildings gradually and safely, so that around 25% of staff can be back on site at any one time. We still expect the vast majority of staff to work from home where possible – this will not change for some months yet – but the roadmap sets out when and how specific buildings will reopen and what our new working environment will look like.


I want to be clear that this plan is just that – a plan. As we have seen, the situation is still evolving rapidly. The local lockdown announced in Leicester last week, and the possibility of lockdowns in some London boroughs, show us that we cannot yet take our safety for granted. It is vitally important that we take reopening one step at a time and ensure that we can adapt as the situation changes. I make no apology for treading carefully at this stage.


The safety of our staff and students is, and will remain, my top priority. That is why in our pilots to date we have prioritised safety and prevention measures onsite. These include hand-washing and social distancing, additional cleaning, the use of face coverings, Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) where appropriate, and trialling temperature checks at the Student Centre, which we’re now rolling out across campus. We have learned a lot from these pilots and from the experiences of colleagues who have been part of the first phase of reopening. It’s clear that the challenge of returning to on-site activity requires a lot of leadership from local teams, who understand best how their working environment operates, with a central framework in place to support that leadership.


I have visited campus twice now, and although it was a little eerie walking around the empty streets of Bloomsbury, it was wonderful to be on campus again, to see familiar landmarks and to meet and chat (from a distance) with staff and students. It was good too to see Jeremy Bentham in his new sealed Perspex home, totally COVID-19-protected – he must have seen it all coming!   


More seriously, it has been inspiring to see local departments so keen to return and willing to give the return plans such time, challenge and consideration. I am grateful to colleagues in the Cancer Institute, Chemistry, Institute of Neurology and Medical Research Council Laboratory for Molecular Cell Biology (MRC LMCB) for taking part in these initial pilots and for showing me how their laboratories work with social distancing in place. I would like to thank everyone involved in the pilots for taking such a dedicated and rigorous approach to all aspects of our planning. The complex operational practicalities of getting campus up and running again can’t be underestimated, and I have been immensely reassured to see how this is all coming together in practice. My thanks also to Ian Dancy and Duncan Palmer and their teams for their exemplary and outstanding leadership and delivery of this very complex project.


I’d also like to thank all the staff in the Student Centre for my two visits there which gave me a real feel for the impact of social distancing on our learning spaces for students. The start of the next academic year certainly isn’t going to be easy, but with teamwork, care and collegiality, I am confident that we can make it work.


It’s also vital that our students – as well as their parents and carers – feel confident that UCL is putting their safety first. That’s why, although the government has announced its “1 metre plus” approach to social distancing, we have confirmed that we will continue to use a 2 metre guideline for timetable planning for Term 1. Indeed, the Chief Medical Officer made it very clear that the official advice is that we should all adhere to 2 metres social distancing wherever we can and only move to “1 metre plus” when absolutely necessary. This is also reflected in the current guidelines from Public Health England (PHE). We hope that this will give us flexibility in the face of Government and PHE advice changing and/or if there are future outbreaks of COVID-19 in London or a second wave nationally.


Our Keeping Safe on Campus webpages include resources and information about commuting, health and safety, and more – such as supporting disabled staff returning to campus. On this last point, I would like to reiterate the importance of understanding the needs of our whole community when planning a return to campus, and this includes colleagues with a disability that is not immediately obvious. With this in mind, UCL has recently signed up to the Hidden Disabilities Sunflower scheme, which allows colleagues to discreetly signal to others that they may need additional help.


I have spoken to many colleagues across academic departments and professional services and it’s clear that there is a spectrum of feelings about a return to campus. There are some who can’t wait to get back into their labs and libraries, and those who miss working with colleagues nearby. At the same time, some are concerned about their journey into work, or caring for loved ones who might be vulnerable. And there are many who will have mixed feelings, or who will be worried about how they will combine caring responsibilities with their professional roles over the coming months. The initial results from our recently launched staff wellbeing survey illustrates just how much of a concern the prospect of a return to commuting is for many of our staff. Our planning is taking all of this into account, and we will do whatever we can to support you through this gradual return to UCL.


Finally, I recognise how hard everyone is working to get us ready for next term. I don’t underestimate the scale of the task, nor the impact it is having on staff in a range of different circumstances and roles. It’s never easy to find time to take a break, but there’s no doubt this year that we will all need to ensure we take opportunities to do so where we can. I have therefore decided to add some additional closure days to this year’s calendar. The first will be on Friday 28 August, before the bank holiday on Monday 31 August 2020. I hope that this will give a couple of days of respite for us all from emails and meetings, and allow everyone a little more time to switch off and regroup before the new academic year begins. Recognising how different Term 1 will be, and how hard you will be working for our students and colleagues over the coming months, I will also be adding three additional closure days at Christmas (21–23 December), so that we can all plan for an extended break at the end of the year. UCL will be closed from 5:30pm on Friday 18 December 2020 until 9am on Monday 4 January 2021.


Please continue to take care of yourselves, and stay safe.


With very best wishes,


Professor Michael Arthur

UCL President and Provost

3. Education planning for the Autumn Term, 2020–21

In response to feedback from colleagues in a series of Education Town Halls, a position statement on Education planning for the Autumn Term has been prepared. This statement emphasises the guidance shared to date to help staff prepare their teaching and assessment activities for the coming term, in line with the Temporary Operating Model for 2020–21.


In Term 1, UCL recommends a ‘digital first’ approach (a mixture of asynchronous and synchronous learning), with some campus-based face-to-face as well as lab- and studio/practice-based teaching activity for students, led by academic or teaching staff. Decisions around the specifics of teaching continue to lie at the local level with individual programme teams, in liaison with the new support infrastructure of faculty and departmental representatives. The statement highlights the key considerations staff should take into account in their Term 1 planning in relation to:


  • planning face-to-face activity;
  • assessments and examinations;
  • Tier 4 visas requirements and implications of wholly online programmes;
  • asynchronous, synchronous and dual-mode learning;
  • process and scheduling of timetabling and rooming; and
  • communicating with students.


We continue to update the Education Planning, 2020–21 pages with new resources, case studies and Frequently Asked Questions, and central teams such as Arena, Digital Education and UCL Library Services are on-hand to support colleagues in preparing for the start of term.


Our Education Town Halls series continues on Thursday 9 July, 10am, with a focus on Laboratory and practice-based education, followed by Timetabling on Friday 10 July, 9am – find out how to join the Town Halls.


If you have any questions or feedback for the Teaching Continuity and Education Delivery Team, please contact VPEDU.teaching.continuity@ucl.ac.uk.
