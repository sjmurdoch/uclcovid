1. Remote working and returning to campus

The Prime Minister’s announcement yesterday that some coronavirus restrictions are being eased this week has left many of us asking “What does this mean for me?” We want to set out here what this means for all of us at UCL: now and over the coming days and weeks.  


We are asking everyone not to return to UCL for the time being, unless you are one of our key staff who have been on-site throughout the lockdown period. Everyone’s safety is paramount and there is much to do to make sure the campus is as safe as possible before larger numbers are able to return.   


The Government has released a guidance document today and we will look at this carefully alongside the plans we are putting in place for a measured and phased return to campus.


The first stage of reopening will comprise a small number of pilots, where we will consult with colleagues in specific areas to identify opportunities to start working on campus again, most likely in June. This may involve particular labs or research areas where it is very difficult or impossible to work from home, and where we are confident that we can operate safely. We will use what we learn from these first pilot schemes to inform the eventual reopening of the university in phases.


We know that many staff would like to return to campus as soon as possible. However, it is important that we do this in a careful and planned way, and we ask you to bear with us as we work through a phased plan and ensure we can prioritise safety at every stage.


We also recognise that the prospect of returning, even for a pilot, will be challenging for many. Please be assured that no one will be compelled to return to UCL for the pilots. Transport is a challenge which is outside our control, and we know that many will not have a viable alternative route to work. We are also aware that many of you continue to have caring and childcare responsibilities that require you to remain at home. Equally, if you don’t feel comfortable participating in a pilot for health reasons, you will not be expected to do so.


We are continuing to monitor the situation here at UCL, taking into account the national picture as the pandemic evolves and the Government position becomes clearer. If the rate of infection (R) rises again nationally, then we acknowledge that we may have to row back on this cautious reopening plan.


The coronavirus restrictions have forced many of you to pause your work and so a big thank you for your continued patience while we find the safest way to welcome you back to campus. We will share more information with you as soon as it is available.


In the meantime, please continue to check the coronavirus information hub for the latest information about UCL’s response to the pandemic, including answers to frequently asked questions for staff.


If you have a specific question or concern about your individual circumstances, please speak to your line manager in the first instance. If you have a more general question that hasn’t been answered online, you can submit a question via this form and we will add the answers to the UCL website.


2. UCL’s Coronavirus: The Whole Story podcast – new episode available now

Coronavirus: The Whole Story is UCL’s weekly podcast, exploring the pandemic from the perspective of researchers across UCL.


Presented by writer, broadcaster and UCL graduate Vivienne Parry, each episode looks at the coronavirus outbreak through a different lens, covering history, psychology, social sciences, arts, engineering, economics, law and politics. Episode 4 is out now, looking at the pandemic’s long-term effect on schoolchildren’s learning. What can you do to make home schooling work better for your child – and for you? And what will it mean for teachers and children when children eventually return to school?


You can listen on the UCL Minds website or wherever you find your podcasts. If you’d like to be part of a future episode – or if you've got a question you'd like a UCL researcher to answer – we’d love to hear from you. Please get in touch by emailing minds@ucl.ac.uk.


3. Open Access resources

Open Access and resources from home

A wide range of Open Access content is available in Explore, in addition to the resources that the Library already provides. It lists all of the content from UCL Discovery, the Research Data Repository and UCL Press, which between them contain freely available versions of papers, books and datasets produced by members of academic staff at UCL.


There are also tools that allow you to search the web for free and legally available articles. The recommended tool is Open Access Button – you can find more about it on the UCL Library Services blog.


Open Access and your research

Don’t forget that you can use both the Research Publications Service (RPS) and the Research Data Repository (RDR) to take advantage of Open Access to share all of your research outputs, as well as pre-prints services like bioRxiv and medRxiv to share early versions of your work.


Research IT Services (RITS) are also available to support you by providing free advice and consultancy to help you to make the best use of IT when managing or carrying out research.


4. Exploring UCL’s campus virtually

Although campus remains closed for now, you can still do some virtual exploring this month, with online events from UCL East and UCL Culture.


Tomorrow (12 May) sees the launch of a new series of virtual Lunch Hour Lectures – the first one discusses contemporary public art at UCL East, looking at how UCL is working with local artists to inspire our community and our neighbours alike.


UCL Culture have launched a monthly online exploration of UCL’s Museums and Collections. The theme for this month is ‘micro worlds’, celebrating the tiny things in our collections from a 3,000 year old wheat genome to our collection of Protozoa. There’ll be weekly ‘micro-meditation’ challenges on social media and you are invited to create your own ‘Digital Micrarium’ using photographs and drawings. You can find all the details on the UCL Culture website.

6. Support for staff and students

If you feel unwell and think that you may have coronavirus, please follow the latest Government advice. You can also find more information on the NHS website.

If you are worried about coronavirus or your wellbeing more generally, please speak to your line manager or check our list of resources to support staff during the outbreak. You can also find useful tips about looking after your wellbeing or if you’re feeling anxious on the Every Mind Matters website.

You can access free counselling services via the Employee Assistance Programme, Care First, which is available 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. Please telephone 0800 197 4510 to speak to an independent professional counsellor or information specialist in confidence.

Please advise any students who are worried to contact askUCL.
